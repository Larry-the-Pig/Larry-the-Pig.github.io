Branches of Government

There are Three Branches of Government

  • The Legislative Branch
  • The Executive Branch
  • The Judicial Branch

The Legislative Branch

The legislative branch, or congress, consists of the House of Representatives, and the Senate.
The House of Representatives has Representatives from each state based on population.
The Senate has 2 senators for each state no matter the population.
Representatives and senators are elected by the people from their state.
The House and the Senate can pass laws, and prevent each other from passing those laws.

The Executive Branch

The executive branch is for the president, vice president, and government/independent agencies such as the FBI, or Department of Agriculture.
The president is considered the head of state and head of government.
The president can veto bills, however congress can overpower a veto if voted for.
The electoral college elects the president.
The president is also the commander-in-chief, and can declare war.

The Judicial Branch

The judicial branch is for courts and the supreme court.
The supreme court's job is to interpret the constitution and decide cases and controversies based on it.
The supreme court can decide that laws are unconstitutional and get them removed, creating a precedent for future government officials.
The supreme court judges are chosen by the president, and have the job for life.
